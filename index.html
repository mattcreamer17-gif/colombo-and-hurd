02 The frame, applied to the petition practice

The firm runs on three kinds of work. Each one belongs on a different layer.

The mistake most firms make is to buy an AI tool that tries to do all three at once. The mistake Colombo & Hurd is less likely to make, because Nease is in the room, is to let adjudicator judgment drift away from human review.

60%
Structured document work

Evidence assembly, exhibit indexing, cross-reference against the three-prong Dhanasar framework, form population, version control across 300+ open matters. Traditional software and database work. A modern practice management layer should handle this, with automation where it earns its keep.

30%
Rule-based workflow

Deadline calculation, priority-date tracking, RFE response templating, bar-ethics checks. Rules and workflow logic. Not an AI problem.

10%
The actual AI-shaped question

Reading an adjudicator's reasoning. Shaping a narrative that threads a client's profile through national interest. The judgment call on whether to file, defer, or restructure. This is where a thoughtfully built orchestration layer earns its place, under attorney supervision.

Case reference

Eduba built a multi-agent compliance system for VigilOre that collapsed 160+ hours of evidence assembly into under five minutes.

The legal supervision stayed with the attorneys. The orchestration layer handled the document mechanics. The structure Eduba used to do that is called Interpretable Context Methodology. It organizes a firm's institutional knowledge as a layered folder system that an agent can draw from, and that a human can audit. 04 The methodology, and the partner stack

The paper

Interpretable Context Methodology

Agent context organized as a layered filesystem. Every generation step ties back to a named source folder the attorney can inspect. Submitted to ACM TiiS. Relevant to bar-ethics review because the interpretability is structural, not asserted.
